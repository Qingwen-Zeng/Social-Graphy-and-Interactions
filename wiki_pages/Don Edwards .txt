'''William Donlon Edwards''' (January 6, 1915 – October 1, 2015) was an American politician of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] and a member of the [[United States House of Representatives]] from California for 32 years in the late 20th century.

==Early life==
Edwards was born in [[San Jose, California]] to [[Republican Party (United States)|Republican]] parents. His father and grandfather owned a [[land title]] business.<ref name=MercuryNews /> After graduating from [[San Jose High School]], Edwards earned a [[bachelor's degree|B.A.]] from [[Stanford University]] in 1936, where he was member of the Stanford golf team. Edwards then attended [[Stanford Law School]]<ref name=latimes/> and was admitted to the [[State Bar of California|bar]] in 1940.

Edwards was a special agent for the [[Federal Bureau of Investigation]] from 1940 to 1941, when he joined the [[United States Navy]] as a naval intelligence and gunnery officer during [[World War II]]. In 1950, he was elected president of the California Young Republicans, resigning after joining the [[United World Federalists]].<ref name=MercuryNews /> Perceiving the Republican Party as becoming too conservative, he became a [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democrat]] prior to his first congressional election victory in 1962.<ref name=latimes/><ref name=NYT /> He was the president of Valley Title Company of [[Santa Clara County, California|Santa Clara County]] from 1951 to 1975, and a delegate to the Democratic National Conventions of 1964 and 1968.

==United States representative (1963–95)==
Edwards was elected as a member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] to the [[88th United States Congress|88th]] from the 10th Congressional District (later redistricted to the 16th Congressional District) and to the fifteen succeeding Congresses (January 3, 1963 – January 3, 1995). In his first year in the House, Edwards voted to abolish the [[House Un-American Activities Committee]]. Edwards was involved in the passage of the [[Civil Rights Act of 1964]] and the [[Voting Rights Act of 1965]]. Edwards was a member of the [[House Judiciary Committee]] during the investigation of the [[Watergate scandal]]. Edwards opposed the U.S. military involvement in the [[Vietnam War]], the [[United States invasion of Panama|invasion of Panama]], and the [[Persian Gulf War]].<ref name=latimes>[https://www.latimes.com/local/obituaries/la-me-don-edwards-20151003-story.html Obituary], latimes.com; accessed October 3, 2015.</ref>

Edwards was one of eight members of the Judiciary Committee to vote for all five articles of impeachment drafted against President [[Richard Nixon]] during the Watergate scandal. The others were [[Jack Brooks (American politician)|Jack Brooks]], [[Robert Kastenmeier]], [[John Conyers]], [[Barbara Jordan]], [[Charles Rangel]], [[Elizabeth Holtzman]] and [[Edward Mezvinsky]]. Three of the five articles were adopted prior to Nixon's resignation on August 9, 1974.

Edwards was one of the [[House impeachment managers]] appointed by the House of Representatives in 1988 to conduct the [[Federal impeachment trial in the United States|impeachment trial]] of [[Alcee Hastings]], judge of the [[United States District Court for the Southern District of Florida]]. However, he was replaced as an impeachment manager before the trial started. However, in 1989, he was appointed and served as a House impeachment manager in the impeachment trial of [[Walter Nixon]], judge of the [[United States District Court for the Southern District of Mississippi]].<ref>https://library.cqpress.com/cqalmanac/document.php?id=cqal89-1138585{{dead link|date=December 2016 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=https://history.house.gov/Institution/Impeachment/Impeachment-List/ |title=List of Individuals Impeached by the House of Representatives |publisher=[[United States House of Representatives]] |access-date=January 15, 2020 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191218232339/https://history.house.gov/Institution/Impeachment/Impeachment-List/ |archive-date=December 18, 2019 |url-status=live}}</ref> Edwards was the chairman of the House Subcommittee on Civil Liberties and Civil Rights for 23 years. Edwards was not a candidate for reelection to the [[104th United States Congress|104th Congress]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://lofgren.house.gov/biography/|title=About|date=December 3, 2012}}</ref> Santa Clara County Supervisor [[Zoe Lofgren]], one of his former aides, won a crowded Democratic primary for the seat and still holds it today.

==Personal life and death==
Don Edwards was married three times;<ref name=NYT>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2015/10/03/us/politics/don-edwards-congressman-who-championed-civil-rights-dies-at-100.html|title=Don Edwards, Congressman Who Championed Civil Rights, Dies at 100|work=[[The New York Times]]|date=October 2, 2015|last1=Clymer|first1=Adam}}</ref> he was married to Edith Wilkie Edwards from 1981 until her death in 2011.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mercurynews.com/2011/04/04/herhold-edie-wilkie-a-life-well-lived/|title=Herhold: Edie Wilkie: A life well lived|work=mercurynews.com|date=April 4, 2011}}</ref> He [[centenarian|turned 100]] in January 2015.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mercurynews.com/scott-herhold/ci_27267905/ex-u-s-rep-don-edwards-turns-100|title=Herhold: Ex-U.S. Rep. Don Edwards turns 100|work=mercurynews.com|date=January 6, 2015|access-date=October 3, 2015}}</ref> He died later that year on October 1, 2015.<ref name=MercuryNews>{{cite web|url=https://www.mercurynews.com/2015/10/02/former-rep-don-edwards-1915-2015/|title=Former Rep. Don Edwards: 1915–2015|work=San Jose Mercury News|date=October 2, 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160128054747/http://www.mercurynews.com/bay-area-news/ci_28912287/former-rep-don-edwards-1915-2015|archive-date=January 28, 2016|access-date=July 25, 2018}}</ref>

==Legacy==
Edwards received the Congressional Distinguished Service Award in 2003.<ref name=latimes/> The [[Don Edwards San Francisco Bay National Wildlife Refuge]] in the south end of [[San Francisco Bay]] is named in his honor.
